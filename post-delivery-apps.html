I drove for three delivery apps for 30 days. Here's what actually paid.

Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Instacart, tracked hour by hour — including the gas, the dead miles, and the one night that made the whole month worth it.

I didn't do this because I thought delivery driving was a smart side hustle. I did it because every "how much can you really make" article I found online was either written by the apps themselves or by someone who drove for one weekend and extrapolated. So I ran all three, back to back, for a full month, and logged everything.

The short version: I cleared $1,240 after gas, before taxes, across roughly 61 hours behind the wheel. That's about $20.30/hour — which sounds fine until you remember there's no benefits, no unemployment insurance, and you're putting real miles on a car you'll eventually have to repair.

The setup

Same city, same 10-mile radius, same car — a 2018 Civic that gets about 34 mpg. I split the month into three 10-day blocks, one app at a time, driving dinner shifts (5–10 PM) plus a few weekend lunch shifts. I logged every mile with a mileage tracker app and every payout as it hit my account, not the "estimated earnings" the app shows before you accept a delivery.

Instacart's gross pay looked the best on paper, but grocery runs meant more idle time in stores and more miles per order, which quietly ate the margin. DoorDash had the best per-hour rate mostly because of one specific stretch — more on that below.

The night that skewed everything

On a Friday during a local college's parents' weekend, DoorDash was running a promo that stacked peak pay on top of already-high base pay. I made $187 in four hours, more than a third of my entire DoorDash total for the month, on that single night. If I pulled that night out of the data, my DoorDash net/hour drops from $21.48 to about $15.60.

One good night can hide a mediocre month.

This is the part most "I made $1,000 in a week" posts leave out. They're usually describing their best week, not a representative one. If you're deciding whether to try this, plan around the $15–20/hour range, and treat anything above that as a bonus, not a baseline.

What the apps don't put in front of you

  • Dead miles. The distance you drive to reach the pickup, and back toward your zone after drop-off, isn't part of the advertised payout — but it's real gas and real wear.
  • Vehicle depreciation. The IRS standard mileage rate (67¢/mile in 2026) is meant to cover gas, maintenance, insurance, and depreciation combined. I put about 890 miles on the car this month. At that rate, my "true" cost was closer to $596, not the $191 in gas alone — which cuts my real net down to roughly $644 for the month, or about $10.55/hour.
  • Self-employment tax. None of this income has taxes withheld. Set aside 25–30% if you're doing this seriously.
What I'd actually recommend
A basic mileage-tracking app that logs trips automatically (not the delivery app's built-in tracker) is the single highest-leverage $0–$5/month tool for anyone doing gig work — it's the difference between guessing your real hourly rate and knowing it. I used Stride, which is free and exports a clean spreadsheet at tax time.

So is it worth it?

As a bridge income while you're between things, or stacked on evenings you'd otherwise be scrolling your phone anyway — yes, reasonably. As a long-term plan, the math gets tougher once you account for full vehicle costs. It paid better than I expected on good nights and worse than the apps imply on average nights, which is probably the most honest thing I can say about it.

Next month: I'm running the same experiment with freelance writing gigs picked up through cold outreach, to see how the hourly math compares once there's no vehicle involved at all.
