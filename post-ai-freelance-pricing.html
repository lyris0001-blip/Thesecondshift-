Price and pitch your freelance work without guessing

Most freelancers underprice out of fear, not math. Here's a six-step workflow to scope the job, price it properly, and pitch it in an email that doesn't sound desperate.

Work through these in order. Copy each prompt into Claude, ChatGPT, or whichever AI you use, and fill in the brackets with your real numbers.

Step 1: Define the scope before you price anything

Vague scope is the #1 reason freelance jobs go over budget. Nail this down first.

Prompt
A client wants me to do [describe the project, e.g. "redesign their small business website"]. Help me turn this into a clear scope of work. Ask me clarifying questions I should get answered before quoting, then draft a scope document with: - What's included - What's explicitly NOT included (to prevent scope creep) - Number of revision rounds included - Estimated timeline

Tip: Send the "what's not included" list to the client before you start. It's the single biggest thing that prevents a job from quietly doubling in size.

Step 2: Find your real market rate

Don't pull a number out of the air — and don't just copy whatever a friend charges.

Prompt
I'm a freelance [your skill, e.g. "graphic designer," "copywriter," "web developer"] with [X years] of experience, working with [type of client, e.g. "small local businesses"]. Give me: - A typical hourly rate range for someone at my experience level and niche - A typical flat-project rate range for a job like [describe project] - 2-3 factors that would justify charging at the top vs. bottom of that range

Tip: Treat this as a starting range, not gospel — rates vary a lot by region and niche. Use it to sanity-check your gut number, not replace it.

Step 3: Calculate your actual rate

Work backward from what you need to earn, not just what feels fair.

Prompt
Help me calculate a freelance rate. My numbers: - I want to earn $[X] per month from freelancing - I can realistically work [X] billable hours per week (not counting admin, marketing, unpaid time) - I want to set aside [X]% for taxes since nothing is withheld Walk me through the math to get to a minimum hourly rate, and explain what I should do differently if the number seems too high for my market.

Tip: "Billable hours" is always fewer than you think — admin, invoicing, and finding clients all eat into the week without pay.

Step 4: Write the pitch or proposal

This is where confidence in the price actually shows up — or doesn't.

Prompt
Write a freelance proposal email for [project], priced at $[amount]. The client is [describe them, e.g. "a small business owner, non-technical, budget-conscious"]. Structure it as: 1. A brief line showing I understood their problem 2. What I'll deliver, in plain terms 3. The price and timeline, stated plainly with no apology or hedging 4. A clear next step (e.g. "Reply to confirm and I'll send over a contract") Keep it under 150 words. No filler, no over-explaining the price.

Tip: Notice the instruction to avoid "apologizing" for the price — AI will hedge by default unless you tell it not to, and so will you if you're not careful.

Step 5: Prepare for pushback

Have your response ready before a client says "that's more than I expected," not after.

Prompt
A client responded to my $[amount] quote saying it's more than they expected. Help me draft 2 response options: 1. One that holds the price firm and explains the value, without being defensive 2. One that offers a smaller/reduced scope at a lower price, so they can choose Keep both under 100 words, confident tone, no apologizing for the original price.

Tip: Having a reduced-scope option ready (not just a discount) protects your rate while still giving the client a way to say yes.

Step 6: Lock it in properly

A verbal "sounds good" isn't a contract. Get the basics in writing before starting work.

Prompt
I'm about to start a freelance project for [project] at $[amount]. Give me a checklist of what should be in writing before I begin work, covering: - Payment terms (deposit, milestones, final payment) - What happens if the client goes unresponsive mid-project - What happens if scope changes after we start - Basic language to protect against late payment This is general guidance, not legal advice — I'll have a lawyer review anything final.
Tested with
Claude and ChatGPT — both handle this workflow well. The pricing-math prompt in particular benefits from giving real numbers rather than round ones; more specific inputs get sharper answers.

Once you land the client, the next AI workflow worth building is a simple check-in and invoicing routine — keeping the relationship (and the payments) on track without extra admin overhead. That's a good topic for another post.
